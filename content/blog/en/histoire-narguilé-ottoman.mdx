Some objects embody an entire civilization. For the Ottoman world, the narghile is one of them. For more than three centuries, from the sixteenth century until the empire's fall in 1922, the narghile was a social marker, a diplomatic tool, a symbol of power and conviviality. Its history is as rich as that of the empire that carried it to its zenith.

---

## The origins: a disputed invention

The exact origin of the narghile remains the subject of unresolved historical debate. Two main theories compete.

**The Indian theory:** According to historian Cyril Elgood, the narghile was invented in India in the sixteenth century, during the reign of the Mughal emperor Akbar. The physician Hakim Abul Fath Gilani is said to have designed the first water-filtration device to make tobacco — newly introduced to India by the Portuguese — less harmful to the Shah.

**The Iranian/Persian theory:** Other scholars place the invention in Persia, where the word "nargil" (referring to the coconut, used as the first base) is of Persian origin. The device was then said to have been refined as trade with the Indian subcontinent intensified.

**The Ottoman convergence:** Whatever its precise origin, it was in the Ottoman Empire that the narghile was elevated to its most elaborate form and acquired its unique cultural status.

---

## The narghile in Ottoman society

The narghile arrived in Constantinople (Istanbul) around 1600, a few decades after tobacco was introduced from the New World via the Europeans. Its spread through Ottoman society was lightning-fast.

**In the palaces:**  
The sultan and his court quickly adopted the narghile as an object of prestige. Imperial narghile pieces were genuine works of art — bases of Bohemian crystal, stems of chased silver, hoses sheathed in silk embroidered with gold. Some pieces, preserved today at the Topkapi museum in Istanbul, reach levels of craftsmanship that would rival the crown jewels.

The sultan had a "narguiléchi" — a personal servant whose sole duty was to prepare and present the imperial narghile. It was an honorary position at court.

**In the coffeehouses (kıraathaneler):**  
Ottoman coffeehouses, which appeared in Constantinople in the 1550s, quickly became the great gathering places of the people's narghile. Hundreds of patrons would meet there daily to play board games, listen to storytellers (meddah), and smoke the narghile.

These coffeehouses were also political spaces — places of debate, where the news was discussed and movements were organized. This political role would eventually worry several sultans, who tried to shut them down. Without lasting success.

**In the hammams:**  
The narghile even found its way into the Turkish baths. After bathing, patrons would settle into the cooling room (soğukluk) to smoke while drinking tea or coffee. The combination of bath, tea, and narghile formed a complete wellness ritual that would endure for centuries.

## The question of prohibition

The history of the narghile under the Ottomans was not a straight line. Sultan Murad IV (1623-1640) banned tobacco on pain of death — an absolute prohibition, narghile included. It is said that he personally patrolled the streets of Istanbul in disguise to catch offenders in the act.

The ban was lifted by his successor Ibrahim I (1640-1648), himself a great lover of the narghile. This back-and-forth of prohibitions and repeals reflects the constant tension between conservative religious authority (some ulema considered tobacco illicit under sharia) and the cultural reality of a practice deeply rooted in society.

## The narghile as a diplomatic tool

In Ottoman diplomacy, the narghile played a codified, symbolic role. Offering the narghile to a visitor was a gesture of welcome and respect. Refusing the narghile was, depending on the era and the context, potentially perceived as an affront.

European ambassadors in Constantinople regularly mentioned the narghile ritual at official audiences in their correspondence. The French ambassador in the eighteenth century noted that "the Grand Vizier receives no guest without the narghile being presented within the first minutes of the audience."

It was partly through this diplomatic channel that the narghile spread to European courts — an Oriental curiosity brought back by fascinated travelers.

## The craft of the Ottoman narghile

The Ottoman Empire developed a narghile craftsmanship of extraordinary richness.

**The glass bases**: Produced in the workshops of Beykoz (on the Bosphorus), they first imitated Bohemian crystal before developing a distinctly Ottoman style — bases blown in cobalt blue, ruby red, and emerald green, sometimes heightened with gilding.

**The metal stems**: The coppersmiths' workshops of the Grand Bazaar produced hand-engraved stems with calligraphic, floral, and geometric motifs. The Ottoman arabesque flourished on stems just as it did on the facades of mosques.

**The hoses**: Sheathed in silk, velvet, or Cordovan leather, quality hoses were finished with amber mouthpieces — the most precious of materials to the Ottomans, who believed in its antiseptic virtues.

**The cases and accessories**: The narghile traveled in wooden chests inlaid with mother-of-pearl. Charcoal carriers, tongs, screens — every accessory was an opportunity for artistic expression.

## The fall of the empire and the narghile's survival

The First World War and the fall of the Ottoman Empire (1922) profoundly transformed Turkish society. Mustafa Kemal Atatürk, founder of the Turkish Republic, pushed modernization at full speed: Western dress, the Latin alphabet, secularization. In this context, the narghile — a symbol of the old Ottoman society — was viewed with suspicion by the modernizing elites.

It did not disappear. It took refuge in the working-class coffeehouses, in the old quarters, among ordinary people. And it survived.

From the 1990s onward, the narghile experienced a spectacular worldwide revival. This was not a return to the past — it was a reinvention. The Ottoman narghile, with its aristocratic codes and hours of solitary contemplation, became the modern hookah: festive, social, shared among friends in trendy lounges in New York, Paris, or Dubai.

---

## The legacy

When you light your hookah today, you are taking part in a ritual that has crossed four centuries, several empires, and dozens of cultures. The gesture — placing the charcoal, drawing gently, watching the smoke rise — is the same as that of an Ottoman janissary in a Constantinople coffeehouse, a Cairo merchant in the souks of the eighteenth century, a Beirut diplomat in the 1960s.

It is this quiet continuity that makes the hookah so special. In a fast-changing world, it remains an unchanging gesture.
